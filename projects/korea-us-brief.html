Why the FX backdrop drives everything

The central tension of the 2026 Korea–U.S. capital relationship is that the money is being pushed one direction by policy while the currency pulls it the other way. Seoul has committed $350B to U.S. industries under the November 2025 trade deal, but the won's weakness means every dollar deployed abroad is more painful — which is precisely why the deal caps outflows at $20B per year to protect the onshore FX market.

Meanwhile, that same weak won makes Korean assets a bargain for U.S. private equity. Blackstone, KKR, Carlyle, and Bain are actively bidding on Korean beauty, pharma, and semiconductor-equipment targets. The result is a genuinely bidirectional market: state-directed Korean capital flowing into U.S. manufacturing, and opportunistic U.S. financial capital flowing into Korean corporate assets.

03 · Directional Outlook Two Theses, Two Directions
🇰🇷 Korea → U.S. · Building, Not Just Buying
Korean capital is increasingly industrial, strategic, and reluctant.
The defining Korea→U.S. story is no longer portfolio investment — it is factories tied to tariff avoidance. Hyundai's $7.6B Georgia Metaplant, Samsung's $37B Texas chip complex, and LG's battery network were all structured to manufacture inside U.S. borders and dodge tariffs. But the cracks are showing: Samsung's Taylor fab is delayed to 2026 with subsidies cut 26%, and the September 2025 ICE raid on Hyundai's Georgia site strained the relationship. The new $350B pledge is best read as strategically coerced capital — committed to avoid 25% tariffs, capped to protect the won, and explicitly permitted to fund projects that "lack commercial rationality."
🇺🇸 U.S. → Korea · Opportunistic & Financial
U.S. private equity sees a cheap, restructuring-rich market.
The U.S.→Korea flow is the mirror image: financial, opportunistic, and accelerating. With the won at multi-decade lows and Korean conglomerates under pressure to divest non-core assets, firms like Blackstone, KKR, and Carlyle have set up permanent Seoul offices. 2025 K-beauty M&A alone hit $1.4B. The targets cluster in beauty, pharma distribution, and the crown jewel — semiconductor-equipment maker HPSP ("Korea's ASML"), with a stake valued near $1.5B drawing every major global fund. Korea's deep pension-driven capital pool and steady corporate restructuring make it, in Deloitte's framing, both a capital source and a destination simultaneously.
