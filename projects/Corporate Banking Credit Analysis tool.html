Corporate Credit Analysis Tool · Built by Benjamin Min · UCR Finance
Implied ratings are illustrative, generated by a transparent scorecard based on published rating-agency methodologies. Not an actual credit rating and not lending advice.

Methodology & Disclaimers

This tool estimates a borrower's creditworthiness from its financial statements using a transparent, weighted scorecard. It is modeled on the structure of public rating-agency methodologies (S&P, Moody's) and standard corporate-banking credit analysis, but it is an educational approximation — not an actual credit rating.

The Five Cs framing

Bank credit analysis traditionally weighs the Five Cs: Capacity (cash flow vs. obligations), Capital (leverage and equity cushion), Conditions (industry / macro), Collateral, and Character. This model quantifies the first three from reported financials; Collateral and Character require qualitative diligence beyond statement data.

Scorecard weights

Leverage — Net Debt / EBITDA30%
Coverage — EBITDA / Interest25%
Cash flow — FCF / Total Debt20%
Profitability — EBITDA margin15%
Liquidity — Current ratio10%

Each metric is scored 1–10 against threshold bands, then blended into a composite that maps to an implied rating (AAA → D). Investment grade is BBB and above.

Industry adjustment

Leverage thresholds shift by industry. Stable, asset-heavy sectors (utilities, real estate, telecom) tolerate higher leverage for a given rating; volatile or asset-light sectors (technology, energy E&P, discretionary retail) require lower leverage for the same rating.

Covenants & capacity

The covenant package shown is a representative corporate facility (max leverage, min interest coverage, min DSCR, min current ratio) for illustration; real covenants are negotiated per deal. Debt capacity shows incremental senior debt available before net leverage crosses each threshold, holding EBITDA and cash constant.

Limitations

  • Single-period analysis; real underwriting uses multi-year trends and projections.
  • No adjustment for operating leases, pensions, or off-balance-sheet items.
  • Net income is derived as (EBIT − Interest) × (1 − tax rate).
  • Not investment, credit, or lending advice.
